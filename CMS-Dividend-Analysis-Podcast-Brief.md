# Cutting My Child Maintenance to Zero: The Regulation 50 Playbook for a 50/50 Father of Three

## A Deep-Dive Briefing on UK Child Maintenance, 50/50 Shared Care, a Disabled Child, and a Step-Son Raised From Two Months Old

**Prepared:** 31 May 2026
**Subject:** A real-world analysis of how a UK paying parent on a genuine 50/50 shared-care arrangement — with two biological children including one with autism, and an 11-year-old step-son raised as his own since infancy — can use Regulation 50 of the Child Support Maintenance Calculation Regulations 2012 to reduce, and potentially eliminate, Child Maintenance Service liability. Includes an immediate band-correction worth about one hundred pounds a month, an honest assessment of which legal levers work and which don't, and a step-by-step action plan.

---

## The Situation in Plain English

Meet Elliot. He's a director of a UK limited company called Select Lifestyles Limited. He is not a shareholder of that company. That distinction matters enormously and it changes the shape of this whole analysis. Because he holds no shares, he cannot legally receive dividends from Select Lifestyles, and that means the classic director's tax-restructuring trick — paying yourself a small salary and topping up with dividends — simply isn't available to him at his current employer.

Elliot's gross salary is fifty-nine thousand five hundred pounds a year, paid as straight PAYE through Select Lifestyles. He also gets a company car valued at two thousand two hundred pounds a year as a benefit in kind. His take-home pay after tax, National Insurance, a small pension contribution and a charity donation is three thousand five hundred and seventy-one pounds and sixty-three pence per month.

There are three children in his life. Oakley is seven years old, born thirty-first of March 2019. Harrison is five, born twelfth of February 2021. Harrison has been diagnosed with autism, and he receives Disability Living Allowance at the middle or higher rate for the care component. The third child is Shae, who is eleven. Shae is biologically the son of Elliot's former partner Leah and a man Shae has never met. Elliot has raised Shae from the time Shae was two months old. Shae calls Elliot Dad. Shae's surname was changed by deed poll to Elliot's surname. Elliot is not on Shae's birth certificate, and Shae's biological father has never had Parental Responsibility.

The custody arrangement for all three children is exactly fifty-fifty, on a fortnightly cycle. In Week One — when it's Elliot's weekend — he has the children Wednesday night, Thursday night, Friday night, Saturday night and Sunday night, dropping them at school on Monday morning. In Week Two — when it's Leah's weekend — Elliot has Wednesday night and Thursday night. Across fourteen days, that's seven nights with Elliot and seven with Leah. Across a year, that's one hundred and eighty-two and a half nights — comfortably above the one hundred and seventy-five night threshold that the Child Maintenance Service uses for what it calls Shared Care Band Equal.

The arrangement is formalised in a written agreement drafted by Matthew Saunderson, Senior Associate at Mills and Reeve LLP, on behalf of Elliot. The agreement covers all three children together. Crucially, Mills and Reeve characterise the arrangement on the face of the letter as "shared care as opposed to contact" — which is the language a Regulation 50 application wants to lean on, because shared care implies equal day-to-day involvement rather than visiting access.

The Child Maintenance Service has assessed Elliot at seventy-two pounds and fifty-five pence per week. That's three hundred and fourteen pounds and thirty-eight pence per month. The assessment was effective from twentieth of January 2026, sits on Direct Pay (Elliot pays Leah directly with no CMS collection charge), and applies only to Oakley and Harrison — Shae is not a qualifying child for CMS because Elliot is not his biological or adoptive parent. The assessment is based on a historic 2024/25 income figure of forty-one thousand three hundred and seventy-five pounds.

Leah receives Child Benefit for all three children. She also receives Harrison's entire DLA award. Elliot receives neither.

Elliot has historic household arrears of six thousand one hundred and eighty pounds — water, council tax, gas and electric — relating to the former family home. These are real obligations but they have no bearing on the CMS calculation.

This briefing answers one question, across multiple layers: how does Elliot get his Child Maintenance to zero, what does he do today to start saving money immediately, how do Harrison's autism and Shae's place in the family strengthen the legal argument, and which apparent legal levers turn out not to work?

## Part One: The Two Errors in the Current Assessment

Before discussing any strategy, we start with what's already wrong on the CMS file.

### Error one: the night count is wrong

The CMS letter at Step Six — shared care — states that Oakley stays with Elliot "three nights a week, a hundred and fifty-six to a hundred and seventy-four nights a year". The letter says the same for Harrison. Based on that count, the CMS has applied the three-sevenths reduction band.

The actual schedule produces seven nights out of fourteen — exactly fifty percent — or one hundred and eighty-two point five nights per year. That's above the one hundred and seventy-five night threshold for Shared Care Band Equal, which uses a half-plus-seven-pounds-per-child reduction.

Working the figures with the historic income on file. Yearly gross income forty-one thousand three hundred and seventy-five pounds. Weekly gross seven hundred and ninety-three pounds and forty-nine pence, just inside the basic rate band. Sixteen percent for two children equals one hundred and twenty-six pounds and ninety-six pence per week. Current three-sevenths reduction gives seventy-two pounds and fifty-five pence per week — that matches the letter.

Under the correct Band Equal reduction: halve to sixty-three pounds and forty-eight pence, subtract fourteen pounds for the two children, equals forty-nine pounds and forty-eight pence per week. That's two hundred and fourteen pounds and forty-one pence per month.

Fixing the band saves about one hundred pounds a month. It's administrative, not legal. Log on to the CMS online account, use "Report a change to shared care", upload the Mills and Reeve agreement, spell out the fortnightly cycle. The new assessment should land within four to six weeks.

### Error two: the income figure is stale and about to bite

The CMS letter uses forty-one thousand three hundred and seventy-five pounds. Elliot's current payslip shows a fifty-nine thousand five hundred pound salary. That's a forty-four percent jump, well past the twenty-five percent threshold under Regulation 35 that would trigger a switch from historic to current income.

That means the CMS will revise upward as soon as HMRC's data feed catches up or Leah prompts a reconsideration. At fifty-nine thousand five hundred per year, the gross weekly income is eleven hundred and forty-four pounds and twenty-three pence, moving Elliot into the basic-plus band. Sixteen percent of the first eight hundred is one hundred and twenty-eight pounds; twelve percent of the three hundred and forty-four pound twenty-three pence slice above eight hundred is forty-one pounds and thirty-one pence; pre-shared-care total one hundred and sixty-nine pounds and thirty-one pence. Apply correct Band Equal: halve to eighty-four pounds and sixty-five pence, minus fourteen pounds, equals seventy pounds and sixty-five pence per week. Three hundred and six pounds per month.

The band correction at the current income figure saves a hundred pounds a month. The same correction at the higher income only saves about ten pounds a month. The window to lock in the larger saving is short. Report the shared-care change today. Do not volunteer the higher income mid-cycle.

## Part Three: The Legal Architecture of Regulation 50

The Child Support Maintenance Calculation Regulations 2012 — statutory instrument 2012/2677 — is the rulebook the CMS works to. Within it, Regulation 50 deals with shared-care cases. It does one specific thing: it tells the CMS when a parent who is physically sharing care should still be legally treated as the non-resident parent for the purposes of paying maintenance.

The key wording: a person is to be treated as a non-resident parent "if, and only if, that person provides day-to-day care to a lesser extent" than the parent with care.

"If and only if" cuts both ways. If you provide day-to-day care to a lesser extent than the other parent, you're the non-resident parent. But if you provide day-to-day care to an equal or greater extent, you are not the non-resident parent, and the CMS has no jurisdiction to assess you. The case closes.

That's the door this briefing is opening. To zero out maintenance, Elliot doesn't need to argue that Leah is a bad parent or that he's a better one. He needs to show his provision of day-to-day care is not lesser. Equal is enough.

### The Child Benefit presumption

The reason this isn't already happening — the reason the CMS has accepted Elliot as the non-resident parent despite the fifty-fifty schedule — is a related rebuttable presumption inside the same regulation. The parent who receives Child Benefit is presumed, "in the absence of evidence to the contrary", to provide day-to-day care to a greater extent.

Leah currently receives Child Benefit for all three children. The presumption is doing the heavy lifting in keeping Elliot classified as the non-resident parent. The phrase "in the absence of evidence to the contrary" is an invitation to bring evidence.

Where two parents both claim Child Benefit for the same child, HMRC applies the priority rules in Schedule 10 of the Social Security Contributions and Benefits Act 1992. For a child genuinely living equally with both parents, HMRC arbitrates on the facts. Importantly, Child Benefit cannot be split for the same child — only one person can be entitled at any one time. But where there are multiple children, Child Benefit can be split on a one-each basis. Leah taking Oakley's claim while Elliot takes Harrison's and Shae's, or any combination, is legally possible.

### What "day-to-day care" actually means

The regulations don't give a precise statutory definition. That's good and bad — good because there's no rigid checklist to fail, bad because the CMS caseworker has wide discretion. The leading authority is *PG v HMRC and NG* [2016] UKUT 216 (AAC), a tax credits case whose principles are routinely applied to analogous main-carer determinations. The Upper Tribunal said overnight stays are "not a trump card … but one factor". The tribunal must collect information about all aspects of responsibility, resolve conflicts in evidence, and reach a balanced judgment.

In practice the factors weighed include: overnight stays, school drop-offs and pick-ups, morning routines, after-school care, bedtime routines, who takes time off work when a child is sick, medical appointments, school emergency contact arrangements, parents' evenings and school correspondence, activities and clubs, clothing and equipment, holidays, decision-making on education and healthcare, and emotional availability.

If most of these factors are equal or weighted toward Elliot, the Child Benefit presumption falls and Regulation 50 closes the case.

## Part Four: How Harrison's Autism Transforms the Argument

When one of the children has additional needs, the analysis under Regulation 50 changes character. Day-to-day care is no longer a generic concept measured by school runs and meals. It includes a whole category of disability-specific work — work that takes more hours, more attention, and more specialist understanding than ordinary parenting.

That additional work is exactly the kind of evidence that defeats the Child Benefit presumption. Because the presumption is rebuttable on the basis of evidence to the contrary, and because disability-specific care is by its nature both effortful and documented, it gives Elliot a richer evidence base than most paying parents have available.

Elliot provides for Harrison's autism by: maintaining sensory equipment in his home, including a weighted blanket, sensory toys, specialist bedding, fidget aids and a quiet space; structured routines — visual schedules, regulated transitions, consistency in transitions; specialist communication tools tailored to Harrison's individual needs; and attendance at autism-specific appointments — paediatrician, occupational therapy, speech and language, CAMHS, EHCP reviews. The care hours during his contact time exceed thirty-five per week, which is the time-and-care threshold the welfare-rights system uses for Carer's Allowance purposes — though Elliot cannot claim Carer's Allowance himself because his fifty-nine thousand five hundred pound salary blows the one hundred and ninety-six pound weekly earnings cap. The thirty-five hour figure remains useful as evidence in the Regulation 50 argument.

When a tribunal hears that one parent is providing more than thirty-five hours per week of qualifying disability care, while receiving none of the DLA award that exists to support that care, it's hearing something quantitatively different from a normal "I see the kids half the time" claim.

### The DLA point

Leah receives the entire DLA award for Harrison. Elliot receives none of it. That fact cuts in two directions and it matters to be clear about both.

The unfavourable direction first: DWP awards DLA on the basis of the child's needs, but the appointee receiving payment is treated by the system as the person principally responsible for managing those needs. The fact that DWP appointed Leah is, to some degree, administrative evidence that aligns with the Child Benefit presumption.

But the favourable direction is more powerful. Elliot is providing equal autism-specific care without receiving any of the financial support that's been awarded for that care. Half of Harrison's autism-related needs are being met at Elliot's home — out of Elliot's pocket, with no benefit support. That's a striking factual position to put in front of a tribunal: he's doing the work without the reward, and Leah is receiving the reward without doing all the work.

### What the law doesn't give you

A few things the research has confirmed are not available, and it's important to know them so you don't waste effort.

DLA cannot be split between separated parents. The leading authority is *R (Barber) v Secretary of State for Work and Pensions* [2002] EWHC 1915 (Admin). DWP can only appoint one person under Regulation 43 of the Social Security (Claims and Payments) Regulations 1987. So you can't ask DWP to pay half the DLA to each parent; you can only ask DWP to substitute the appointee from Leah to Elliot. There's no published statutory right of appeal for a non-recipient parent to do this against the existing appointee's wishes. Practically very difficult unless Leah agrees.

CMS has no variation for the qualifying child's disability. Special expenses variations under Regulations 63 to 67 of the 2012 Regulations cover only the paying parent's own household, including "relevant other children" living with the paying parent. Harrison's disability does not by itself trigger any reduction in the standard calculation. Elliot cannot say to CMS "I spend money on autism adaptations for Harrison, please reduce my maintenance".

Carer's Allowance is blocked by the earnings cap. Elliot's net weekly earnings of roughly eight hundred and twenty-five pounds are well over the one hundred and ninety-six pound cap. Even though he meets the thirty-five hour test and Harrison's DLA is at the qualifying rate, he can't claim it.

The disability dimension doesn't unlock a separate cash-saving route. It does something more valuable: it provides the evidence base that makes the Regulation 50 case-closure argument materially stronger than it would be for an ordinary 50/50 case.

## Part Five: Shae — The Step-Son and Why He Strengthens The Case

Shae is the most powerful piece of evidence in the entire Regulation 50 file, even though — perhaps because — he doesn't appear in the CMS calculation at all.

### Shae is not a qualifying child for CMS

Section 54 of the Child Support Act 1991 defines a "parent" for CMS purposes as a person who is the biological or adoptive parent of the child. Elliot is neither in relation to Shae. So Shae is not a qualifying child. CMS has no jurisdiction to assess Elliot to pay maintenance for Shae, and CMS has not done so. That's the right outcome legally.

### Shae cannot currently be claimed as a "relevant other child" either — and here's exactly why

The "relevant other child" provision is the mechanism by which a paying parent's gross weekly income gets reduced before the maintenance percentages are applied. The reduction is eleven percent for one relevant other child, fourteen percent for two, and sixteen percent for three or more. If Shae counted as Elliot's relevant other child, that would carve eleven percent off Elliot's gross weekly income before the calculation runs, producing a further saving on top of the band correction.

But the statutory definition is closed. Paragraph 10C(2) of Schedule 1 to the Child Support Act 1991 says a relevant other child is either (a) a child for whom the non-resident parent or their partner *receives* Child Benefit, or (b) such other description as may be prescribed. Regulation 77 of the 2012 Regulations prescribes the only two extensions: a child outside Great Britain (irrelevant), or a child for whom an election under section 13A Social Security Administration Act 1992 has been made not to receive Child Benefit (the workaround high earners use to avoid the High Income Child Benefit Charge — also irrelevant here).

There is no provision that captures a child for whom the paying parent "would be entitled but for another person's existing claim". There is no provision for de facto step-parenthood. There is no provision based on the child living with the paying parent. Receipt of Child Benefit by the paying parent (or their current partner) is, with two narrow exceptions, the only key that unlocks the relevant other child reduction.

So as the position stands today, Shae does not give Elliot any reduction in his CMS calculation.

### The one way to change that — Child Benefit transfer

The only way to bring Shae into the calculation as a relevant other child is for Elliot to take over Shae's Child Benefit from Leah through HMRC's priority dispute process. Schedule 10 of the Social Security Contributions and Benefits Act 1992 sets out the priority rules. Where two parents claim, HMRC weighs the facts: which parent the child lives with, who provides day-to-day care. In a genuine 50/50 case, HMRC arbitrates and the priority is contested.

If Elliot succeeded in taking over Shae's Child Benefit, Shae would immediately count as a relevant other child — eleven percent income reduction before percentages. The same principle would apply if he took over Harrison's Child Benefit (the disability evidence is particularly strong for Harrison). Both children at once would be sixteen percent reduction.

The risk of a Child Benefit claim is that Leah would experience it as a financial attack and that may prompt her to escalate elsewhere. The reward is that even if HMRC rules against Elliot on the priority question, the contested status itself weakens her Child Benefit presumption under Regulation 50.

### Where Shae actually does the work — the Regulation 50 evidence

Shae's place in Elliot's home is the strongest piece of day-to-day-care evidence in the entire file. The factual position is striking:

He has lived with Elliot since the age of two months. Elliot has been Shae's only father figure for the whole of his life. Shae calls Elliot Dad. Shae bears Elliot's surname by deed poll. Shae's biological father has never met him and has no Parental Responsibility. Elliot pays for and takes Shae to football training and matches every week. Shae is named in the same Mills and Reeve agreement as Oakley and Harrison and operates on the same 50/50 schedule.

The Regulation 50 test is about day-to-day care in fact, not biological parenthood. A parent who voluntarily takes on a third child — not biologically his, with no legal obligation to do so — and raises that child as his own for eleven years, is not doing "lesser" day-to-day care than the other parent. He's doing more.

This is exactly the cumulative, undeniable, factual evidence that defeats the Child Benefit presumption for Oakley and Harrison. A tribunal hearing the Shae facts will struggle to find that Elliot's day-to-day care of any of the children is to a "lesser extent" than Leah's.

### Parental Responsibility — what to check

You said the Mills and Reeve document is a written agreement, not a sealed court order. That matters legally. Under section 12(2) of the Children Act 1989, where a Child Arrangements Order made by the court names a non-parent as a person with whom the child is to live, that person automatically acquires Parental Responsibility. But this only operates on a court-issued and sealed order, not on a private solicitor-drafted agreement, however thorough.

So in legal-PR terms, you almost certainly do not currently have formal Parental Responsibility for Shae. You could acquire it by applying to the Family Court for a "lives with" Child Arrangements Order naming you in respect of Shae, which would then automatically confer PR. The application requires permission under section 10 of the Children Act, but in a case where you've raised the child for eleven years and are named in a private agreement, permission would be very likely.

Whether to pursue that PR application is a separate question from the CMS strategy. The argument for doing it: it puts your legal standing for Shae beyond doubt, which strengthens any future medical, educational or welfare decision-making. The argument against: it's a court process that costs time and money and is not strictly necessary for the Reg 50 CMS argument, which runs on factual day-to-day care evidence regardless of whether you hold formal PR.

The recommendation is to pursue it in parallel with the CMS strategy, not as a precondition. The Reg 50 application can be made on the current facts.

## Part Six: Running the Argument — Sequence

### Move one: fix the band today

Log onto the CMS online account. Use "Report a change to shared care". Upload the Mills and Reeve agreement, including the email correspondence that updates the term-time schedule. Spell out the fortnightly cycle: Week One five nights with Elliot, Week Two two nights with Elliot, seven nights out of fourteen, one hundred and eighty-two and a half nights per year, in the one-hundred-and-seventy-five-or-more band. Ask for the calculation to be redone using Shared Care Band Equal.

This is administrative. CMS should action it on the documents. New assessment in four to six weeks, around two hundred and fourteen pounds per month — a hundred-pound monthly saving for however many months pass before the income recalculation catches up.

### Move two: build the evidence file

The Regulation 50 argument runs on documentary evidence. The file should contain three categories.

**General parenting evidence for all three children:** Signed Mills and Reeve agreement; written updates including the weekend-parent-does-Monday-drop change; school letters confirming you as equal emergency contact and attendance at parents' evenings; GP and dental records with your address; a twelve-month calendar showing actual overnight stays for all three children; receipts and bank statements for clothing, uniforms, shoes, food, activities, holidays; date-stamped photographs of day-to-day care.

**Disability-specific evidence for Harrison:** Harrison's DLA award letter (request via Subject Access Request to DWP if you don't have a copy); the current draft EHCP; evidence you are named on or consulted for the EHCP; receipts for sensory equipment and autism-specific items at your home; appointment letters and attendance records for paediatrician, OT, SLT, CAMHS, EHCP reviews; a one-page written description of the structured routines you maintain.

**Step-son evidence for Shae:** Deed poll documentation showing the surname change; school records showing Elliot as a parent contact; GP and dental records; receipts for football club fees, kit, transport; a short narrative covering Shae's history — that you have been the only father figure since age two months and that his biological father has never been involved; the Mills and Reeve agreement showing Shae included on the same 50/50 footing.

The Mills and Reeve "shared care as opposed to contact" framing should be highlighted in any covering document and quoted directly in the Reg 50 application.

### Move three: file the applications — in this order

**First, file the Regulation 50 application with CMS.** Cite Regulation 50 by name. Apply to be treated as a person with care rather than a non-resident parent on the basis that you provide day-to-day care to no lesser extent than Leah. Attach the evidence file. Specifically reference Harrison's autism evidence and Shae's facts. Cite *PG v HMRC and NG* [2016] UKUT 216 (AAC) for the proposition that overnight stays are one factor among many and that all aspects of responsibility must be weighed. Ask for the maintenance assessment to be ended.

**Second, in parallel, file Child Benefit claims with HMRC for at least one of the children — most cleanly for Harrison.** Leah keeps Oakley's and Shae's claims. A one-each split is legally clean and easier to defend than a full transfer. If HMRC rules in your favour, Harrison's CB transfers to you. That serves two purposes: it removes the CB presumption for Harrison under Reg 50, and (if the band correction is already in place) it makes Harrison a relevant child only for you, which has implications for the Reg 50 case. An optional further move is to claim Shae's Child Benefit as well, which would also bring Shae into the calculation as a relevant other child — but this is a heavier confrontation and should be sequenced after the first applications have been responded to.

**Third, as an optional move, apply for a "lives with" Child Arrangements Order from the Family Court in respect of Shae.** This confers formal Parental Responsibility under section 12(2) Children Act 1989 and removes any ambiguity about your legal standing. This is a Family Court application, separate from the CMS process, and would benefit from family-law-specialist representation.

**Pause on the DLA appointee challenge.** The previous draft of this briefing suggested it as an option. On reflection, the evidence-generation value can largely be achieved by Subject Access Request, and the relational cost of applying to displace Leah as Harrison's DLA appointee outweighs the modest probability of success.

If any application is refused, file a mandatory reconsideration within thirty days, then appeal to the First-tier Tribunal if needed.

## Part Seven: Realistic Outcomes

Three scenarios.

**Best case — full Reg 50 success.** CMS accepts the equal day-to-day care argument across all three children. The case closes. Maintenance falls to zero. Annual saving against the current three hundred and fourteen pound monthly figure: three thousand seven hundred and seventy-two pounds. Probability moderate-to-strong, materially boosted by the autism evidence and Shae's facts.

**Middle case — Harrison's Child Benefit transfers to Elliot, full Reg 50 partial success.** The Child Benefit presumption flips for Harrison and the Reg 50 argument succeeds for him; Oakley's element remains, possibly reduced because the disability evidence pushes the day-to-day care assessment in Elliot's favour for the sibling group as a whole. Outcome could range from one hundred and fifty pounds per month down to zero depending on the tribunal's view.

**Worst case — band fix only.** Both presumptions survive. Monthly saving about a hundred pounds at current income, locked in for as many months as pass before the income recalculation. Annual saving five hundred to twelve hundred pounds depending on timing.

The floor in every case is the band correction — a virtually certain win because it's a documentary error CMS will fix on production of the agreement. Everything above is incremental upside. The autism dimension and Shae's facts together act as a probability multiplier on the best and middle scenarios.

## Part Eight: Watch-Outs

A handful of practical points.

**Don't volunteer the higher income mid-cycle.** The current assessment uses forty-one thousand three hundred and seventy-five pounds. Don't volunteer the current salary; the annual review will pull it in automatically when the time comes.

**Document the updated drop-off rule.** The weekend-parent-does-Monday-drop arrangement should be in writing. A WhatsApp confirmation is enough. Without it, Leah's solicitor could insist the original Mills and Reeve schedule governs.

**Don't characterise applications as retaliatory.** The CMS and Tribunal are alert to anything that looks like a points-scoring exercise. Frame every application — CMS, HMRC, Family Court — as factual and focused on the children's welfare.

**Subject Access Requests are powerful and free.** Send SARs to: the children's school (records of emergency contacts, parents' evening attendance, SEN correspondence), GP and dental practices (appointment records), the Local Authority SEN team (EHCP draft and correspondence about Harrison), and DWP (Harrison's DLA award letter, any correspondence). These produce documentary evidence at no cost.

**The Child Benefit claim for Shae is the highest-friction move.** It signals to Leah that the financial position is being actively challenged. Sequence it after the initial Reg 50 application has been responded to.

**The household arrears are separate.** Six thousand one hundred and eighty pounds on water, council tax and gas/electric have no bearing on CMS. Address them in a parallel budget. Prioritise the gas and electric at forty-five hundred pounds — enforcement risk including warrants of entry is highest there.

## Part Ten: The Spirit of the Law

The Child Maintenance Service exists to ensure that children whose parents live apart are financially supported. Nothing in this briefing is about avoiding that responsibility.

Regulation 50 exists because the legislators recognised that in a genuine fifty-fifty shared care arrangement, both parents are already providing for the children directly — through their homes, their food, their clothes, their time, their attention. Imposing a maintenance assessment on top of that means the paying parent contributes twice.

Elliot's case is striking. He spends equal nights with his two biological children, covers half their food bills, half their bath nights, half their school runs on his days. He attends parents' evenings, takes them to the dentist, pays for their swimming and football. He maintains an autism-adapted environment for Harrison out of his own pocket while every penny of Harrison's DLA goes to Leah. And he has raised Shae — Leah's son from a previous relationship, biologically not his — as his own from infancy, given him his surname by deed poll, and continues to provide for him on the same 50/50 footing as the two biological children.

His financial contribution to the children's lives is already substantial and continuous — it just doesn't pass through Leah's bank account. Regulation 50 says, in those circumstances, the CMS shouldn't be in the middle of it.
