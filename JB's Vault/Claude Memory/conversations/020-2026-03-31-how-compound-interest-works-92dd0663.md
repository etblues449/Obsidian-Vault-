**Compound interest** = earning interest on your interest, not just your original amount.

---

**Simple vs compound — the key difference**

Say you invest £10,000 at 7%:

| Year | Simple interest | Compound interest |
|------|----------------|-------------------|
| 1 | £10,700 | £10,700 |
| 5 | £13,500 | £14,026 |
| 10 | £17,000 | £19,672 |
| 20 | £24,000 | £38,697 |
| 30 | £31,000 | £76,123 |

With simple interest, you earn £700/year forever. With compound, that £700 gets added to your pot — so next year you earn interest on £10,700, then on £11,449, and so on.

---

**The formula**

`Final value = P × (1 + r)^t`

- P = principal (starting amount)
- r = annual rate (as a decimal, so 7% = 0.07)
- t = years

So £10,000 at 7% for 20 years: `10,000 × (1.07)^20 = £38,697`

---

**The "Rule of 72"**

Divide 72 by your interest rate to get roughly how many years it takes to double:

- 7% → doubles every ~10 years
- 10% → doubles every ~7 years

---

**Why time matters more than rate**

Starting 10 years earlier often beats getting a higher rate. Someone who invests £10,000 at 25 and leaves it until 65 (40 years, 7%) ends up with ~£150k. Starting at 35 gives ~£76k — half as much, despite only 10 fewer years.
